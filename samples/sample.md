# Photosynthesis: How Plants Turn Light into Food

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria
convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. It is the
foundation of nearly every food chain on Earth and the primary source of the
oxygen in our atmosphere.

## The Core Equation

In simple terms, photosynthesis combines carbon dioxide and water, using light
energy, to produce glucose and oxygen:

    6 CO2 + 6 H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6 O2

The glucose stores energy that the plant uses to grow, while the oxygen is
released as a by-product.

## Where It Happens

Photosynthesis takes place mainly in the leaves, inside organelles called
chloroplasts. Chloroplasts contain a green pigment called chlorophyll, which
absorbs light most strongly in the blue and red parts of the spectrum and
reflects green light — which is why leaves look green.

### Chloroplast Structure

- **Thylakoids**: stacked, disc-shaped membranes where the light-dependent
  reactions occur.
- **Stroma**: the fluid surrounding the thylakoids, where the Calvin cycle
  takes place.
- **Grana**: stacks of thylakoids that increase the surface area for capturing
  light.

## The Two Stages

### 1. Light-Dependent Reactions

These reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes and require light. Chlorophyll
absorbs photons, exciting electrons that travel down an electron transport
chain. This process splits water molecules (photolysis), releasing oxygen, and
produces two energy-carrying molecules: ATP and NADPH.

### 2. The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)

The Calvin cycle takes place in the stroma and does not directly require light.
It uses the ATP and NADPH from the first stage to "fix" carbon dioxide into
glucose through a series of enzyme-driven steps. Because it relies on the
products of the light reactions, it cannot run indefinitely in the dark.

## Factors That Affect the Rate

Three main factors limit how fast photosynthesis can occur:

1. **Light intensity** — more light generally means a faster rate, up to a
   saturation point.
2. **Carbon dioxide concentration** — higher CO2 levels speed up carbon fixation.
3. **Temperature** — enzymes work best within an optimal range; too hot or too
   cold slows the process.

At any moment, the slowest of these is the *limiting factor*.

## Why It Matters

Photosynthesis sustains life on Earth in two ways. First, it produces the
organic molecules that feed almost all living things, either directly (plants)
or indirectly (animals that eat plants). Second, it releases the oxygen that
most organisms need to breathe. Understanding photosynthesis is also central to
addressing climate change, since plants absorb carbon dioxide — a major
greenhouse gas — as they grow.
